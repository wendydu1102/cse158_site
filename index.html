The Predictive Task

What exactly are we predicting?

We use machine learning to predict adults’ nightly sleep duration in hours, using demographic, socioeconomic, and health-related variables from NHANES. This is framed as a supervised regression problem.

Target

Average nightly sleep (hours)

Aggregated from weekday and weekend self-reported sleep duration.

Inputs

Demographics & lifestyle

Age, gender, race/ethnicity, poverty-income ratio, work status, exercise, alcohol use, and mental health indicators such as depression frequency.

Evaluation

MAE, RMSE, R² on held-out test set

We compare models against naive and linear baselines and carefully tune hyperparameters via cross-validation.

Plain-language explanation

Each NHANES participant tells us how many hours they usually sleep on weekdays and weekends. They also provide extensive information about their health, work, and habits. Our goal is to teach a model to see patterns in this data so that, given a new person with similar characteristics, it can estimate how long they typically sleep at night.

We measure how far off our guesses are in hours. If the model says "7.5 hours" and the person reports "6.5 hours", that is a 1-hour error. We summarize performance with the average error across everyone in the test set.

Technical formulation
  • Task type: supervised regression, predicting continuous sleep duration.
  • Input features (X): demographic variables (age, gender, race/ethnicity, household size, poverty-income ratio), lifestyle variables (exercise per day, work status, alcohol use), and mental health indicators (depression, appetite).
  • Target (y): Avg_Sleep, the average nightly sleep duration in hours across weekdays and weekends.
  • Baselines: global mean predictor, linear regression on core features, and regularized linear models.
  • Validity checks: train/validation/test split, k-fold cross-validation for hyperparameters, and residual analysis across key subgroups (age, gender, race/ethnicity).

Participants who report more frequent depressive symptoms tend to have slightly more dispersed and sometimes shorter sleep distributions, hinting at a complex relationship between sleep and mental health.

Missingness map

Most variables are fully observed, with missingness concentrated in a few self-reported mental health and alcohol-use questions. Rows with missing target values were removed; remaining missing features were handled via simple strategies during preprocessing.

Modeling

How did we build the models?

We compared a suite of linear and non-linear models, from simple baselines to ensemble methods and neural networks, to see which best predicts sleep duration given our feature set.

Linear baselines
  • Ordinary Linear Regression
  • Ridge Regression
  • Lasso and ElasticNet
  • Pros: interpretable, stable, low risk of overfitting; Cons: limited ability to capture non-linear patterns.
Tree-based & ensemble models
  • Decision Tree Regressor
  • Random Forest & Extra Trees
  • Gradient Boosting & HistGradientBoosting
  • Pros: flexible and non-linear; Cons: more complex, require tuning, less transparent than linear models.
Other models
  • Support Vector Regression (RBF kernel)
  • k-Nearest Neighbors
  • Multi-layer Perceptron (neural network)
  • These methods can, in principle, approximate very complex relationships but are highly sensitive to hyperparameters and data scale.
Preprocessing pipeline
  • Filter to adults (18+) and merge NHANES modules on respondent ID.
  • Drop rows with missing sleep duration; handle remaining missing values using simple imputation strategies.
  • Encode categorical variables (e.g., gender, race/ethnicity, work status) using appropriate encodings.
  • Standardize or scale features where required (especially for SVR, k-NN, and neural networks).
  • Split data into train, validation, and test sets with a fixed random seed; run grid search or randomized search over hyperparameters using cross-validation on the training data.

Evaluation & Results

Key findings from the model comparison

After hyperparameter tuning, we evaluated all models on the held-out test set. Despite modest R² scores across the board, the comparison reveals clear patterns about which methods work best and why predicting sleep is so difficult.

Non-linear & Ensemble Models

Gradient Boosting takes the lead

R²: 0.018 → 0.038 after tuning · MAE decreased noticeably

Gradient Boosting showed one of the most dramatic improvements once its hyperparameters were tuned. It moved from a mediocre baseline to the best overall model, demonstrating that modest non-linear structure in the data can be exploited when the model is configured carefully.

#1 model after tuning
Tuning unlocks non-linear performance
Hyperparameter Sensitivity

SVR, Extra Trees & HistGradientBoosting rebound

Negative R² → top-5 performers after tuning

Support Vector Regression (RBF kernel), Extra Trees, and HistGradientBoosting all started with negative R² scores—worse than simply predicting the mean. After tuning, they rose into the top tier of models, with SVR ultimately becoming the second-best performer.

Their default settings were poorly matched to the dataset, but careful adjustment of depth, learning rate, and regularization transformed them into strong contenders.

Default hyperparameters can be misleading
Linear Models

Stable but quickly outpaced

Minimal change in performance before vs. after tuning

Ridge, Lasso, and ElasticNet regressions changed very little with tuning. In some cases, cross-validation even selected parameters that were slightly worse on the test set—a normal outcome when optimizing averaged fold performance.

These models were among the best before tuning, but they lack the flexibility to capture non-linear patterns. Once tuned, the more complex non-linear models quickly surpassed their performance ceiling.

Reliable baselines, limited expressiveness
Most Improved, Still Struggling

MLP & k-NN show big gains but low R²

MLP R²: −0.2675 → −0.0047 · MAE ↓ by >11%

The neural network (MLP) achieved the largest absolute improvement, moving from essentially unusable to nearly matching the trivial baseline. Yet it remained the second-worst model overall, likely because the dataset is too small and noisy for such a flexible model to learn stable patterns.

k-Nearest Neighbors improved its R² by over 50% but also stayed near the bottom of the ranking, suggesting that distance-based similarity is not a good inductive bias for this feature space.

Big relative gains, modest absolute performance
Overall Difficulty

Sleep is fundamentally hard to predict

All models achieve low R² despite tuning

Even our best model explains only a small fraction of the variance in sleep duration. This suggests that many key determinants of how long people sleep—such as stress, environment, chronotype, shift work, and unmeasured health factors—are missing from the available features.

The result is a sobering one: sophisticated algorithms cannot fully compensate for incomplete data. For predicting sleep duration, observable demographics and basic health metrics only tell part of the story.

Data limitations dominate model choice
Main Lessons

What should we remember?

First, hyperparameter tuning is essential. For non-linear and ensemble models, tuning can transform an underperformer into a top model and is necessary for fair comparison.

Second, the task itself is intrinsically challenging. The consistently low R² scores across all algorithms reveal that the majority of variability in sleep duration simply cannot be captured by the features available in NHANES.

Tuning matters, but data matters even more

Related Work

How does this compare to prior research?

Many NHANES studies use sleep duration as an input to explain health outcomes such as cardiovascular disease, obesity, and mental health. Our project reverses the direction: we attempt to predict sleep from demographic and health features.

NHANES & sleep duration

Prior work has leveraged NHANES to examine associations between short or long sleep and outcomes such as cardiovascular risk, metabolic syndrome, and depressive symptoms. These studies typically treat sleep as a categorical exposure (e.g., short/normal/long) and use logistic or linear regression with carefully selected covariates.

Our findings echo a consistent theme: sleep is correlated with many aspects of health, but the relationships are noisy and heavily confounded by unobserved factors.

Machine learning in sleep research

Recent work applies machine learning to classify sleep disorders, detect apnea from physiological signals, or predict health outcomes from wearable-derived sleep metrics. Compared to those settings, our problem is limited by coarser self-reported features and cross-sectional data, which helps explain the modest performance of even sophisticated models.

By systematically benchmarking a wide range of models on NHANES, we highlight where modern ML adds value and where better data collection is the real bottleneck.
